<p class="" data-start="223" data-end="650">If ambient music were a forest, then <strong data-start="260" data-end="270">Loscil</strong> would be the cryptid quietly sipping artisanal tea under a moss-covered fern, occasionally releasing sound waves that make your spine feel like it's melting—pleasantly. Born <strong data-start="445" data-end="461">Scott Morgan</strong> (but definitely more powerful when called Loscil), this Canadian composer has made a career of sneaking into your brain, laying down a futon of reverb, and whispering, <em data-start="630" data-end="650">“Shhh, just vibe.”</em></p>
<p class="" data-start="652" data-end="1180">Now, most musicians say they’re inspired by life, love, or the emotional intricacies of human existence. Loscil? He once made an entire album inspired by <strong data-start="806" data-end="829">shipping containers</strong>. Yes, containers. Those big, rusty steel boxes that scream “logistics” more than “lyrical.” And somehow, it’s beautiful. You listen to <em data-start="965" data-end="986">“Monument Builders”</em> or <em data-start="990" data-end="999">“Plume”</em> and suddenly you’re not in your apartment eating cold pasta—you’re floating in a fog-drenched Vancouver harbor at midnight, wondering if the foghorn is in love with the lighthouse.</p>
<p class="" data-start="1182" data-end="1572">His music isn’t just ambient; it’s <strong data-start="1217" data-end="1238">glacially ambient</strong>. If Brian Eno created music for airports, Loscil makes music for the parts of your brain you only access when you're brushing your teeth and disassociating. He doesn’t <em data-start="1407" data-end="1413">play</em> songs so much as <strong data-start="1431" data-end="1454">gently unspool them</strong>, like a ribbon in zero gravity. Listening to Loscil is like being hugged by a cloud who has read a lot of philosophy.</p>
<p class="" data-start="1574" data-end="1820">Let’s talk about his album names. They sound like IKEA furniture designed by poets. <em data-start="1658" data-end="1669">“Submers”</em>, <em data-start="1671" data-end="1685">“Sea Island”</em>, <em data-start="1687" data-end="1702">“Equivalents”</em>. You’re never entirely sure if you’re about to hear a song or buy a minimalist lamp. Either way, you will be soothed.</p>
<p class="" data-start="1822" data-end="2208">But here’s the real secret: Loscil is <strong data-start="1860" data-end="1899">ambient music with a sense of humor</strong>—not laugh-out-loud, stand-up-comedy humor, but the kind where you find yourself giggling because the track named “Chinook” genuinely sounds like a snowstorm sighing. There’s a track called <em data-start="2089" data-end="2098">“Ahull”</em>, and if that’s not a sound you can picture a ghost ship slowly doing a yoga stretch to, I don’t know what is.</p>
<p class="" data-start="2210" data-end="2547">Loscil’s superpower is making <strong data-start="2240" data-end="2251">nothing</strong> sound like <strong data-start="2263" data-end="2277">everything</strong>. A single tone loops and you swear it’s the sonic equivalent of your third-grade memories. A tiny click repeats and suddenly you’re emotionally invested in a digital cricket. He could probably mic a toaster and make it sound like the existential ache of Saturn’s moons.</p>
<p class="" data-start="2549" data-end="2808">In a world full of bangers, drops, and algorithmically-enhanced chaos, Loscil is out here like, <em data-start="2645" data-end="2674">“What if we just simmered?”</em> And we do. Oh, how we simmer. We become one with the drones, the delays, the echoes of seismic feelings and hydroacoustic whalesongs.</p>
<p class="" data-start="2810" data-end="2954">So next time you need to study, nap, cry, or astral project to a place where time has no meaning and gravity is optional—<strong data-start="2931" data-end="2941">Loscil</strong> is your guy.</p>
<p class="" data-start="2956" data-end="3063">Just don’t ask him to DJ your birthday party. Unless your idea of fun is watching everyone slowly levitate.</p>
