<p data-start="82" data-end="358">If rock music were a dusty thrift store full of forgotten gems, broken radios, velvet jackets, and acid-stained notebooks, then <strong data-start="210" data-end="242">The Brian Jonestown Massacre</strong> would be the slightly unhinged shopkeeper handing you a tambourine and inviting you to a séance behind the curtain.</p>
<p data-start="360" data-end="753">Founded in the early ‘90s by the infamous <strong data-start="402" data-end="420">Anton Newcombe</strong>, BJM didn’t just form a band — they summoned a swirling, psychotropic <em data-start="491" data-end="499">entity</em>. The name alone is a mischievous mashup of The Rolling Stones' Brian Jones and the Jonestown tragedy — equal parts homage, irony, and warning label. It’s the kind of moniker that says, <em data-start="685" data-end="753">we’re here to blow your mind, and possibly our own in the process.</em></p>
<p data-start="755" data-end="1099"><strong data-start="755" data-end="826">The Brian Jonestown Massacre sounds like time folding in on itself.</strong><br data-start="826" data-end="829" />Think jangling 12-strings from 1967. Think droning sitars, tape hiss, tambourines, Velvet Underground sneers, shoegaze fuzz, and lyrics like transmissions from a parallel universe where Syd Barrett never left Pink Floyd but got way into Eastern mysticism and dub reverb.</p>
<p data-start="1101" data-end="1455">Their early albums — <em data-start="1122" data-end="1134">Methodrone</em>, <em data-start="1136" data-end="1177">Their Satanic Majesties' Second Request</em>, and <em data-start="1183" data-end="1206">Take It from the Man!</em> — are kaleidoscopic opuses of retro-future garage psychedelia. Anton wasn't just channeling the ’60s; he was <strong data-start="1316" data-end="1349">building an alternate version</strong> of it. One where the Summer of Love never ended, but everyone got more cynical and the drugs got weirder.</p>
<p data-start="1457" data-end="1865"><strong data-start="1457" data-end="1523">BJM wasn’t just a band — it was a cult, a commune, a war zone.</strong><br data-start="1523" data-end="1526" />Their story was beautifully, tragically, and <em data-start="1571" data-end="1584">hilariously</em> captured in the 2004 documentary <em data-start="1618" data-end="1624">Dig!</em>, where we watched Anton simultaneously create transcendent music and detonate every bridge within a 5-mile radius. Fistfights on stage? Check. Screaming about artistic purity mid-set? Oh, absolutely. Kicking bandmates out mid-tour? Routine.</p>
<p data-start="1867" data-end="2338">But here's the thing: <strong data-start="1889" data-end="1923">the chaos was part of the art.</strong><br data-start="1923" data-end="1926" />Anton Newcombe isn’t playing to win Grammys. He’s playing to pierce the veil. He’s a mad scientist in a Berlin lab (literally — he moved there), experimenting with frequencies, drones, and drugs of the auditory kind. He treats recording like spellcasting, layering sounds until the air gets heavy with mood. His Bandcamp is a treasure trove of sonic experiments, singles, bootlegs, B-sides, and full-blown epics.</p>
<p data-start="2340" data-end="2663">And still, <em data-start="2351" data-end="2360">somehow</em>, The Brian Jonestown Massacre remains prolific — 20+ albums deep and counting. Each one is a new spellbook: sometimes dreamy, sometimes abrasive, always unmistakably BJM. There’s no nostalgia here; just perpetual reincarnation. They are <strong data-start="2598" data-end="2625">psychedelia’s cockroach</strong>, too wired to die, too weird to stop.</p>
<p data-start="2665" data-end="2957"><strong data-start="2665" data-end="2685">Anton once said:</strong> “I want to make music that’s interesting in 100 years.”<br data-start="2741" data-end="2744" />And honestly? He might. Because while trends come and go, while Spotify algorithms pump out endless beige-core playlists, BJM is still in the basement with a reel-to-reel, making strange, beautiful, <em data-start="2943" data-end="2950">alive</em> noise.</p>
<p data-start="2959" data-end="3177">So light some incense. Drop the needle. Let your third eye dilate.<br data-start="3025" data-end="3028" />BJM isn’t for casual listening. It’s for time travel, ego death, and maybe punching your own reflection while a tambourine rattles in the background.</p>
<p data-start="3179" data-end="3228"><strong data-start="3179" data-end="3228">Get in. Tune out. Massacre your expectations.</strong></p>
