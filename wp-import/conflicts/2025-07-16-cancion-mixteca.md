<span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">In Wim Wenders’ 1984 film Paris, Texas, one of the most affecting moments is non-verbal. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">Ry Cooder’s sparse slide guitar shimmers in the American desert. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">A man’s voice rises to meet it, a song that has no name, at least for the audience. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">As it goes on, the film’s absent father, Travis (Harry Dean Stanton), kneels and looks skyward, as if the sound of that voice had summoned it. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">“Canción Mixteca” (written in 1915 by José López Alavez) spills over the scene in lilting Spanish: It’s a simple line, and it speaks to something base in anyone who has ever felt a little out of place. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">But for immigrants, for the child of immigrants, it’s an open wound.</span>

<span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">The song comes, of course, as Travis starts to come into contact with his history, his life, the person he used to be, a lost soul making his way through the wide, strange expanses of the American desert. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">Travis is not a Mexican, but there is no musical irony in his evocation. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">This is a song about exile, about displacement, about a wound that will not stop bleeding, for any of us.</span>

<span class="text-black dark:text-gray-200">The immigrant is an exile. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">Her family is elsewhere, scattered, sometimes altogether gone. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">She longs to belong to one place, to let that word “home” echo with the same clear tone from inside her heart. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">Her home is a shard in her memory that never quite gets filled in by her new world. </span><span class="text-black dark:text-gray-200">There is no past tense in exile. </span><span class="text-black dark:text-gray-200">The immigrant carries her home with her.</span>

<span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">I didn’t see Paris, Texas at the cinema. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">I saw it years later, after I had already left home. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">Palermo was far behind me, out of reach in time as well as space. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">But there I was, sitting and watching Travis trek through the desert, searching for something he could not quite name, when something inside me cracked open. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">Loneliness, disorientation, the slow pulse of quiet hope. </span><span class="text-black dark:text-gray-200">I recognized it all.</span>

<span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">For the immigrant, Canción Mixteca can be an anthem of belonging. </span><span class="text-black dark:text-gray-200">It is not a nostalgic song. </span><span class="text-black dark:text-gray-200">It aches, it is elegiac. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">It is for someone who can’t go home. </span><span class="text-black dark:text-gray-200">It is for someone who is lost. </span><span class="hover:bg-blue-100 dark:hover:bg-blue-400/30 hover:cursor-pointer text-black dark:text-gray-200" aria-expanded="false">The song pulls at the listener, like memory pulls us back to the bones of things we can’t quite name, not necessarily to joy, but to truth.</span>

With a strangely raw vulnerability within its sound, these words probably mean nothing to most people. For anyone who has left their homeland by choice, necessity, or fate, however, Canción Mixteca is an almost sacred hymn. It is a voice, a voice thousands of hearts would speak in silence, that of those who work, love, and raise families in a country which is not theirs, but continue dreaming in their homeland verbalizations.

This folk song has in fact transcended boundaries to become a transnational anthem. Sung out in Paris, Texas, deserts are bridged-over emotional and literal. To remind that exile is a condition not only political-it's a human one.

And at that moment, somewhere between Texas and Paris, and possibly Palermo and Oaxaca too, Cancion Mixteca sings no less than for immigrants-but for any person that has longed for a place which they cannot touch anymore.
