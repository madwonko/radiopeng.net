<p class="" data-start="251" data-end="269">Imagine a man who:</p>

<ul data-start="270" data-end="504">
 	<li class="" data-start="270" data-end="313">
<p class="" data-start="272" data-end="313">Owned seven identical gray velvet suits</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="314" data-end="354">
<p class="" data-start="316" data-end="354">Signed letters as “a phonometrician”</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="355" data-end="444">
<p class="" data-start="357" data-end="444">Composed piano pieces with directions like “Play like a nightingale with a toothache”</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="445" data-end="504">
<p class="" data-start="447" data-end="504">Once formed his own church (where he was the only member)</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p class="" data-start="506" data-end="671">You’re picturing <strong data-start="523" data-end="537">Erik Satie</strong>, the French composer who strolled into music history with an umbrella, a monocle, and absolutely zero patience for musical pomposity.</p>


<hr class="" data-start="673" data-end="676" />

<h3 class="" data-start="678" data-end="716">🧼 The Cleanest Eccentric in Paris</h3>
<p class="" data-start="718" data-end="1107">Born in 1866 and raised partly by a music publisher and partly by the Jesuits (so... conflicted), Satie looked like a stuffy accountant but thought like a surrealist stoner. He carried a hammer around, just in case, and insisted that everything he ate be white: “white food only” was his personal culinary aesthetic. Rice, eggs, bones, shredded chicken, milk, and moldy bread. Yes, really.</p>
<p class="" data-start="1109" data-end="1218">He bathed regularly (a rarity for 19th-century composers), hated Beethoven with a passion, and once declared:</p>

<blockquote data-start="1220" data-end="1286">
<p class="" data-start="1222" data-end="1286">"I came into the world very young, in an age that was very old."</p>
</blockquote>
<p class="" data-start="1288" data-end="1342">Translation: he was ahead of his time, and he knew it.</p>


<hr class="" data-start="1344" data-end="1347" />

<h3 class="" data-start="1349" data-end="1397">🎹 Gymnopédies: Sadness for Gentle Centaurs?</h3>
<p class="" data-start="1399" data-end="1736">Satie’s most famous works are the <em data-start="1433" data-end="1446">Gymnopédies</em>—slow, melancholic piano pieces that feel like the music a ghost might play while folding linens. They glide, they drift, and they don’t really <em data-start="1590" data-end="1594">go</em> anywhere, which was the point. He wasn’t interested in development or climax. He wanted <strong data-start="1683" data-end="1697">atmosphere</strong>—like musical wallpaper, but very chic.</p>
<p class="" data-start="1738" data-end="1835">🎵 Try this one:<br data-start="1754" data-end="1757" />[🎧<a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eMnxjdGTK4w" target="_blank" rel="noopener"> Gymnopédie No. 1</a> – the official soundtrack of melancholy baguette-walking]</p>
<p class="" data-start="1837" data-end="2010">The titles themselves were obscure. What is a Gymnopédie? Technically, an ancient Spartan dance performed in the nude. Was that Satie’s vibe? We’ll never know. But probably.</p>


<hr class="" data-start="2012" data-end="2015" />

<h3 class="" data-start="2017" data-end="2060">🎪 Playful, Peculiar, Possibly Trolling</h3>
<p class="" data-start="2062" data-end="2189">Satie didn’t just write music—he wrote <strong data-start="2101" data-end="2124">nonsense with flair</strong>. Consider this from <em data-start="2145" data-end="2165">Embryons desséchés</em> ("Desiccated Embryos"):</p>

<ul data-start="2191" data-end="2363">
 	<li class="" data-start="2191" data-end="2239">
<p class="" data-start="2193" data-end="2239">One movement is dedicated to a sea cucumber.</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="2240" data-end="2363">
<p class="" data-start="2242" data-end="2363">Another mocks Beethoven’s "funeral march" by ending in a triumphant flourish labeled “Obligatory Cadenza (by Beethoven).”</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p class="" data-start="2365" data-end="2409">His score directions were famously unhinged:</p>

<ul data-start="2410" data-end="2563">
 	<li class="" data-start="2410" data-end="2433">
<p class="" data-start="2412" data-end="2433">“With astonishment”</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="2434" data-end="2477">
<p class="" data-start="2436" data-end="2477">“Like a nightingale with a stomachache”</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="2478" data-end="2499">
<p class="" data-start="2480" data-end="2499">“Light as an egg”</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="2500" data-end="2533">
<p class="" data-start="2502" data-end="2533">“From the corner of the room”</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="2534" data-end="2563">
<p class="" data-start="2536" data-end="2563">“With invisible enthusiasm”</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p class="" data-start="2565" data-end="2659">The man annotated his music like it was a script for a dream sequence written by Monty Python.</p>


<hr class="" data-start="2661" data-end="2664" />

<h3 class="" data-start="2666" data-end="2691">⛪ The Church of Satie</h3>
<p class="" data-start="2693" data-end="2878">Not content with just being a composer, Satie founded his own religion: <em data-start="2765" data-end="2816">L’Église Métropolitaine d’Art de Jésus Conducteur</em>. He was the <strong data-start="2829" data-end="2837">only</strong> member, bishop, choir, and congregation.</p>
<p class="" data-start="2880" data-end="3031">He issued fierce theological manifestos attacking enemies who were unaware they were part of a holy war. At one point, he referred to one of them as:</p>

<blockquote data-start="3032" data-end="3088">
<p class="" data-start="3034" data-end="3088">“A dirty little underpant-thief of musical depravity.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p class="" data-start="3090" data-end="3134">Never let it be said he lacked poetic range.</p>


<hr class="" data-start="3136" data-end="3139" />

<h3 class="" data-start="3141" data-end="3191">🐣 Furniture Music (a.k.a. Ambient Before Eno)</h3>
<p class="" data-start="3193" data-end="3415">Satie invented <strong data-start="3208" data-end="3227">furniture music</strong>—background music meant to blend into the atmosphere. In one performance, he begged the audience to <em data-start="3327" data-end="3339">not listen</em>. Naturally, everyone sat and stared in rapt silence, which drove him crazy.</p>
<p class="" data-start="3417" data-end="3510">In many ways, he prefigured ambient music by a good 50 years. Brian Eno owes him a croissant.</p>


<hr class="" data-start="3512" data-end="3515" />

<h3 class="" data-start="3517" data-end="3560">🎨 Cubism, Dada, and Pianos in the Rain</h3>
<p class="" data-start="3562" data-end="3622">Satie kept strange, brilliant company. He collaborated with:</p>

<ul data-start="3623" data-end="3860">
 	<li class="" data-start="3623" data-end="3706">
<p class="" data-start="3625" data-end="3706"><strong data-start="3625" data-end="3643">Claude Debussy</strong> (who called him “a gentle medieval monk lost in modern times”)</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="3707" data-end="3757">
<p class="" data-start="3709" data-end="3757"><strong data-start="3709" data-end="3725">Jean Cocteau</strong> (who probably fueled the weird)</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="3758" data-end="3789">
<p class="" data-start="3760" data-end="3789"><strong data-start="3760" data-end="3777">Pablo Picasso</strong> (costumes!)</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="3790" data-end="3860">
<p class="" data-start="3792" data-end="3860"><strong data-start="3792" data-end="3812">Sergei Diaghilev</strong> (Russian ballet impresario with cash and chaos)</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p class="" data-start="3862" data-end="4017">His ballet <em data-start="3873" data-end="3881">Parade</em> included typewriters, foghorns, and pistol shots. One critic called it “a work of idiocy.” Satie wrote him a postcard that said simply:</p>

<blockquote data-start="4019" data-end="4099">
<p class="" data-start="4021" data-end="4099">“Sir and dear friend, you are nothing but an arse. And an arse without music.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p class="" data-start="4101" data-end="4142">He was convicted of defamation. Worth it.</p>


<hr class="" data-start="4144" data-end="4147" />

<h3 class="" data-start="4149" data-end="4180">☂️ The Umbrella in the Room</h3>
<p class="" data-start="4182" data-end="4291">Satie died in 1925, alone in his tiny, dusty Arcueil apartment. When his friends finally entered, they found:</p>

<ul data-start="4292" data-end="4440">
 	<li class="" data-start="4292" data-end="4312">
<p class="" data-start="4294" data-end="4312">Over 100 umbrellas</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="4313" data-end="4362">
<p class="" data-start="4315" data-end="4362">Two grand pianos stacked on top of each other</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="4363" data-end="4409">
<p class="" data-start="4365" data-end="4409">Thousands of scraps of surrealist writings</p>
</li>
 	<li class="" data-start="4410" data-end="4440">
<p class="" data-start="4412" data-end="4440">Velvet suits. So many suits.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<hr class="" data-start="4442" data-end="4445" />

<h3 class="" data-start="4447" data-end="4485">🎼 The Patron Saint of the Strange</h3>
<p class="" data-start="4487" data-end="4610">Erik Satie wasn’t just a composer. He was an aesthetic movement in human form—equal parts Dada, dream, and dadaist dreamer.</p>
<p class="" data-start="4612" data-end="4734">His music whispers instead of shouts. It lingers like perfume. It’s the soundtrack of gentle absurdity and serious whimsy.</p>
<p class="" data-start="4736" data-end="4787">So next time you hear <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PLFVGwGQcB0" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em data-start="4758" data-end="4776">Gnossienne No. 1</em></a>, try this:</p>
<p class="" data-start="4789" data-end="4953">Sit still.<br data-start="4799" data-end="4802" />Light a candle.<br data-start="4817" data-end="4820" />Picture a melancholic platypus waltzing through a foggy Paris street.<br data-start="4889" data-end="4892" />Then raise a glass of milk, in honor of the Velvet Gentleman.</p>
